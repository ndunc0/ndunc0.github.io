About Me

Hi! My name is Nick Duncan. I'm a Software Engineer currently living in Chicago, IL and working at Fanhouse, where I lead mobile app development for our iOS and Android apps. I graduated from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign with a BS in Mechanical Engineering (High Honors).

I was born and raised in the Chicagoland area before moving to Champaign, IL for school. Prior to returning home, I've had the opportunity to live and work in a few different cities including Raleigh, NC; Charleston, SC; Oklahoma City, OK; and Seattle, WA.

Previously, I worked at Pratt & Whitney as a Software Developer, supporting the Engine Health Management Team. Before that, I was a Project Manager for Collins Aerospace, where I owned testing and qualification activities for a touchless lavatory development program. Additionally, I took on a stint as a Project Engineer on a sister program, managing development and release processes for a number of touchless lavatory configurations.

I also spent time as a Repair Design Engineer for the F135 jet engine, authoring 5 repair procedures for the United States Air Force (F-35 Lightning II). Prior to my graduation from the University of Illinois, I was an intern at Boeing (Structures Design & Stress), John Deere (Hydraulics Design), and HydraForce (Hydraulics Design).

In my free time, I like to cook, mess around on my ukulele, and try my best to improve my golf swing.

Plutus - Digital Receipts

       

I developed a mobile app for iOS and Android platforms with the mission to eliminate the use of paper receipts. Using Plutus, a user could generate a virtual email address to use in lieu of a personal email address when opting for digital receipts. All receipts were parsed and appeared in the user's Plutus app. In addition, real trees were planted as a user collected digital receipts, thanks to a partnership between Plutus and Eden Reforestation Projects. F135 Repair Manual

I authored 5 technical, high-priority repairs for Pratt & Whitney's F135 jet engine. I led an IPT to gather requirements and substantiation for my decisions, ultimately allowing for me to write repairs without any new or custom tooling needed. Repairs were largely structures-based and applicable to both CTOL and STOVL configurations.

787 Part Design Change (x2)

I designed 2 new parts to correct supplier non-conforming conditions (tolerancing/interference) affecting 1/3 of the planes during assembly. My designs eliminated the problem and are applicable to all minor models (-8, -9, -10). I designed these parts while in close contact with the supplier to create a design that could be initially retrofitted from existing inventory and thus decrease the overall cost of the change. I also ran stress analyses to prove the structural integrity of the new parts and substantiate new geometry.

QuikTrak Cooling System Redesign

At John Deere, I redesigned the QuikTrak mower's hydraulic cooling system to meet the company's temperature benchmarks for critical components within the system. I worked primarily in Creo to reroute hoses and add/remove different components within the system. I also modeled a prototype, sheet metal part in order to retrofit new components to an existing QuikTrak model for testing. After vigorous testing, various designs, and analyzing different CFD results, I created a system that improved the efficiency of the old system by as much as 18%, and left Deere with a final design that would theoretically add an additional 7.5% improvement over the baseline.
